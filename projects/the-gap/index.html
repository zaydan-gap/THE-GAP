The Question

This program isn't for everyone.
It's for the right people.

Most people will look at what you're about to read and say no. And that's fine — it's not for them. THE GAP is for the ones who've always felt like they were living slightly sideways from their own life. Like there's a version of themselves they haven't met yet.

This is 91 days of doing things that society told you were either too dangerous, too impractical, or too weird — and finding out that the person who emerges on the other side is the one you were supposed to be all along.

This is a group experience. You go through it together — 10 to 20 people. The hard moments hit harder because you're not alone in them. The wild moments become stories you carry forever. The connections formed are forged, not built.

"Know why you're here. Pour everything into it." — The two words beneath everything: Ikigai (your compass) + Meraki (your fuel)
⬇Phase 1 · Days 1–56

Decondition

Strip the noise. Get your body back. Do hard and wild things. Stop performing who you're supposed to be.

👁Phase 2 · Days 57–70

Encounter

Meet masters, elders, guides. See what it looks like when people actually chose themselves. Let it rearrange you.

↑Phase 3 · Days 71–91

Return

Come back as the guide. Lead others. Give it away. Service isn't an add-on — it's the final exam.

🌊Every Day · All 91

The Only Rule

Cold water every morning. Move your body every day. One real conversation with a stranger. Everything else is flexible — these three are not.

How each day is structured: Morning cold immersion → Movement → Main adventure (solo or group) → Shared meal → Open evening. The structure is light by design. The experience is the teacher.

Week 1Days 1–7 · Silence Before the Storm

Arrive. Breathe. Hear Yourself.

Day 1
Solo
Land in the Wild
Group arrives in Auckland, drives straight to remote wilderness baches in Coromandel. No plan. First night: fire, food, ocean. Cold water plunge as the sun goes down. You're here.
Day 2
Solo
2 Hours. No Phone.
Everyone splits off alone with zero agenda. Walk, sit, wade in a river. Reunite for a group dinner and one question: "When did you last feel completely yourself?" No right answers. Just listen.
Day 3–4
Group
Routeburn Track
Multi-day alpine hike, hut-to-hut. 32km. Everyone carries their own pack. The group finds its rhythm. Who helps? Who goes quiet? Who surprises themselves? Mountains reveal people fast.
Day 5
Group
Milford Sound at Dawn
Sea kayak through fjords, waterfalls, ancient cliffs. Start before any other boats are on the water. Total silence broken only by paddles and bird calls. Optional: swim under a waterfall.
Day 6
Group
Wild Swim Day
Drive the west coast. Rule: swim in every blue river you cross. No exceptions. By the time you stop driving, everyone has been in 4–6 different rivers. Dry off on the road.
Day 7
Group
Open Fire Night
Rest day. Group cooks together. Long evening around the fire. Music, stories, no schedule. The first real night of just being together without the world telling you what to do.
"The mountains will tell you who's in the group within 24 hours — faster than any icebreaker ever could."

The Land Knows Who You Are

Day 9
Group
Waipoua Forest + Maori Guide
Full day with a Maori guide in ancient kauri forest. Learn whakapapa — you are not just yourself, you are all the people who came before you. Indigenous answers to questions modern culture forgot to ask.

Group
4-Hour Ocean Kayak

Guide local Maori youth through a trail they've never done. You become the guide for one day. The group leads. No saving — just showing up and moving alongside people.

Group

No headlights. Your eyes adjust within 20 minutes to a sky brighter than you've ever seen in the northern hemisphere. The Southern Cross. The Milky Way as a road. Sit on the summit together in total silence for 15 minutes. No one will want to leave.
Week 3Days 15–21 · Queenstown

Adrenaline Is a Truth Serum

The principle: in the 3 seconds before you jump, you cannot maintain a story about who you are. All masks dissolve. The group uses adrenaline as an instrument — not entertainment.

Day 16
Hard
Bungee — The Original
AJ Hackett, 43m, the first commercial bungee in the world. Group rule: immediately after, before talking to anyone, go somewhere alone and sit with what just happened. 10 minutes. Then come back.
Day 18
Hard
Skydive — 15,000 feet
60 seconds of freefall at 200km/h. No past, no future — just now. That level of presence is available without the plane. The whole program is learning to access it on the ground.
Day 19
Group
Canyon Swing + Whitewater
Canyon swing in the morning, Shotover River rafting in the afternoon. Group builds language through shared chaos. Inside jokes form. This is where friendship happens.
Day 20
Solo
Queenstown Hill — Alone
Everyone hikes up separately. 45 minutes at the summit without a phone. Don't photograph it yet. Just be somewhere you earned with your body. Reunite at the bottom for dinner.

The Quietest Place You'll Ever Go

Days 30–34
Hard + Wild
AIDA Freediving Certification
Group takes AIDA 2 together in Amed. 10–15 meters on a single breath. The ocean rewards calm — which means every anxious thought is a physical liability. Some people will cry after their first deep dive. Don't fight it. It's relief.
Day 36
Group
Uluwatu at Dawn
Surf below a 1,000-year-old clifftop temple. Paddle out before sunrise. When light hits the water, you understand why people gave their lives to the ocean. Group surf all day — wipeouts celebrated equally with rides.
Day 33
Group
Balinese Homestay
Group splits into pairs and stays with local Balinese families in Ubud for 3 nights. Eat their food. Wake to their rhythm. Attend a ceremony if invited. Different world, same humanity.
Day 35
Group
Breathwork + Ice Bath
Wim Hof breathwork session as a group. Round after round, together. Then ice bath challenge — group stays in until everyone's been in. The power was always internal. Today you prove it.
Day 37
Service
Reef Cleanup Dive
The ocean gave you everything this week. Group spends a full morning on a reef cleanup dive with a local conservation org. Service doesn't need to be complicated — show up, do the work, go back in the water.
Day 40
Group
Cliff Jump + Night Swim
Nusa Penida cliff jump. Then nighttime ocean swim — bioluminescence if the season is right. Swimming in water that glows beneath you is one of the most disorienting and beautiful experiences on earth.
Weeks 7–8Days 43–56 · Muay Thai Camp, Thailand

The Ring Teaches What the Classroom Cannot

Days 43–56 · Every Day
Hard
Train Like a Fighter
Morning session (6am): 5km run → shadowboxing → bag work → pad work → technique. Afternoon session (4pm): clinch → sparring → conditioning. No exceptions. Days 4–6, everyone will want to quit. That's the exact moment the program is designed around. Stay. By day 10 you stop thinking and start moving. That shift is the whole point.
Day 45
Hard + Wild
First Sparring
The moment another person's glove connects, your brain's public-image-management system goes completely offline. Who you are in that response — without any filter — is information. Group debriefs together that evening: what did you find out about yourself?
Day 50
Group
The Wall
By Day 8 something shifts. You stop thinking about your hands. The trainer nods once. Your body is learning a new language. The group is visibly different from who arrived. Talk about it.
Day 56
Milestone
Optional White Collar Bout
Whoever wants it gets a fight. You don't need to win. You need to know what it feels like to walk into the ring and not run. The 30 meters to the ring is the whole program condensed.
Days 54–56
Service
Kids at the Camp
Most Muay Thai camps have local kids training. Final 3 days: hold pads, teach footwork, just show up. You came here as students. Now you're guides — for a few days. Notice how that changes you.
"Days 4–6 is the exact moment the program is built around. Choosing to stay when everything says go is when you begin to actually meet yourself."

Walk Where Samurai Walked. No Headphones.

Days 57–60
Group
Nakasendo Trail
4-day walk along the ancient samurai road. Sleep in ryokans. The rule is strict: no headphones, the entire walk. Just footfall on 500-year-old stone, mountain air, and each other. Group conversations on the road go deeper than anything in a sitting circle.
Days 61–63
Group
Japanese Alps
Multi-day alpine traverse. Harder than New Zealand. But you're not the same people who landed in Auckland. Onsen (hot spring) every evening — sore bodies, mountain silence, recovery as ritual.
One Morning
Group
4:30am Temple, Kyoto
Wake before dawn. Walk to a Zen temple in silence. Watch monks begin their morning practice. You're not there to participate — just to observe people who made their whole life a practice. Let that land.
Week 10
Service
Mountain Rescue Volunteers
Spend a day with Japan's mountain rescue volunteer teams — people who give their weekends to keep mountains safe for strangers, with zero pay and zero recognition. Work trail maintenance alongside them. Pure service. No spotlight.
Week 11–12Days 64–70 · Mastery + Fuji

Study How They Hold the Craft

Days 64–68
Group
Apprenticeship Week
Group splits by interest — Aikido, Judo, Kyudo (archery), ceramics, calligraphy. One real sensei. One full week. You're not learning the skill. You're learning how they hold the craft. The patience, the obsession with small things. Stay for that.
Days 68–69
Hard + Wild
Mt. Fuji — Night Ascent
Group climbs through the night. Summit at sunrise — 1,300 years of people climbing this mountain to witness this light. You're in that line. 15 minutes at the top with no phones. Together. Then whatever you need to do.
Days 71–73 (Optional)
Hardest Day in the Program
Zen Sesshin — 3-Day Silent Retreat
For those who want it: a 3-day Zen silent retreat. 12 hours of meditation per day in complete silence. This is harder than Muay Thai. Every story you tell about yourself will surface. Most won't survive. Whoever does it reports it as a turning point. Not required — but for the right person, it's everything.
"You're not studying the skill. You're studying how they hold the craft — the patience, the obsession with small things, the way they inhabit a room."

Weeks 15–16Atlas Mountains + Sahara

The Desert Does Something to People.

Week 15
Group
Atlas Mountain Trek — Berber Guide
Hire a local Berber guide — not a tourist company. His knowledge of the Atlas is 500 years deep. Walk slow. Ask him everything. What the land means to him. What time means to him. Different answers than you're used to.
One Night
Solo
Alone in the Sahara
The group spends one night in the desert — but scattered, alone, out of sight of each other. No tent. Mat, fire, stars. Zero light pollution. The Milky Way bright enough to throw shadow. In the morning: come back and talk. Don't pre-empt the silence with what it should have felt like.
Day After
Group
Morning Circle: What the Desert Showed You
This is the mid-program reset. Halfway through. Nobody comes out of a night alone in the Sahara the same. The group gathers. One round of sharing. No commentary — just listening. The guide closes it: "You're not the person who landed in New Zealand. Keep going."
"You can't be distracted by what's not there. The desert makes the question simple: Who are you when there's nothing left to perform for?"

Service

Medellín has incredible youth programs born from the city's recovery. Group shows up for 3 days — morning workouts, movement sessions, just being present. The rule: come back on Day 2. Anyone can show up once. Showing up again is where trust lives.

Group
Rock Climbing + Parkour
Rock climb in Guatapé. Then find the parkour community — train with locals, not expats. Medellín's movement scene is world-class. An afternoon of movement with people who grew up moving through this city hits different than a gym.
Week 11Coffee Region + Amazon

Slow Down. Then Go Deep.

Days 65–67
Group
Coffee Finca — Wake at 5am
Rent bikes. Ride the mountain roads through the Eje Cafetero. Stay on a working finca. Wake at 5am with the farmers and walk the rows. Understand what it means to pour yourself into the earth every single day for decades. Meraki made physical.

Group
Amazon — Indigenous Community
Go with a small Ticuna indigenous community — not an eco-lodge. Someone's home. River kayaking, jungle expedition, survival basics. Ask about how they understand time, purpose, and belonging. These concepts don't translate — that's the entire point. You'll feel small. It will feel good.

What Comes After

The World School

You finish the program with a lived curriculum, a proof of concept, relationships with local guides across 6 countries, and a story. That's the foundation for running THE GAP for others.

10–20People per cohort

Year 1: Run it yourself. Document everything. The world school isn't built from a pitch deck — it's built from that document you write in Costa Rica on Day 79.

Year 2: Recruit. Partner with every local guide, camp, and instructor you met across 6 months. They get consistent participants. You get a program that didn't exist before you built it.

The pitch to every person you want to bring along:
"You know that feeling — like you're living someone else's life? This is 91 days to find out who's actually in there."
"Most people die with their music still in them.
This is the program that makes you play it."

THE GAP is not a vacation. Not a gap year in the conventional sense. It's 91 days of stripping away the false self and constructing the real one — together, with people who won't let you quit.

The goal isn't a diploma, a credential, or a story to tell at parties.
It's a self you can actually recognize.
