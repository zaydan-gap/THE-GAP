The Philosophy

A fight that looks
like a dance.

Capoeira was created by enslaved Africans in Brazil, beginning in the early 17th century. Forbidden from practicing martial arts by their captors, they disguised combat training as dance. Two people would ginga — a constant side-to-side swaying movement — while the berimbau dictated the tempo and the energy of the game. The kicks and sweeps were hidden in the movement. The weapons were the same as the art.

What resulted was something unique in the history of martial arts: a fighting system that is simultaneously music, dance, acrobatics, and combat philosophy. You cannot separate them. The berimbau tells you how fast to play, how much power to use, how much to trust your opponent. The ginga keeps you in constant motion so nothing is ever a fixed target. The au (cartwheel) and macaco (back handspring) are both evasions and attacks. The roda — the circle of practitioners who clap, sing, and watch — is the theater in which all of this unfolds.

Capoeira Angola, the older style taught at GCAP, is different from the more athletic Regional style practiced in most gyms worldwide. Angola is slower, closer to the ground, more cunning. The Mestre is not trying to impress you with acrobatics — he is trying to read you, confuse you, and invite you to make a mistake. Every movement is a question. Your response is your answer. The quality of the conversation is the quality of the practice.

The Gap · Core Belief
"The roda is 400 years old and it is still going. In the same streets, with the same berimbau, between two people who are trying to understand each other through movement. That continuity — the fact that this is exactly what it was — is the rarest thing in the world."

Every discipline in THE GAP framework is chosen because it teaches something that cannot be learned in a classroom. Capoeira teaches you to read another person's intentions through their body, to disguise your own, and to stay present in the constant negotiation of the roda. These skills — perception, deception, presence, play — transfer everywhere.

The Pelourinho

GCAP — Grupo Capoeira Angola Pelourinho — is based in the Pelourinho district of Salvador, the oldest African-Brazilian neighborhood in the Americas. The Pelourinho is where the slave markets were. It is where the terreiros (Candomblé temples) have been operating for 200 years. It is where Mestre Pastinha ran his famous academy in the 1960s — the same lineage that flows through Mestre Moraes to GCAP today. The streets are cobblestone and color. The music comes from open windows at night. The history is present in the air in a way that does not require explanation.

To train capoeira in the Pelourinho is to train it in the place it was born and where it survived. That is not a backdrop. It is the context that makes everything else mean something.

Pedro Moraes Trindade — Mestre Moraes — founded GCAP (Grupo Capoeira Angola Pelourinho) in 1980 in the Pelourinho district of Salvador. He is a direct student of Mestre Pastinha, the grandmaster who spent his life preserving Capoeira Angola as a distinct tradition when modernizers were trying to replace it with a sportier, more athletic version. Mestre Pastinha died in 1981, nearly blind and broke, in a city that had torn down his academy to build a tourist attraction. Mestre Moraes carries his tradition forward.

GCAP is not a commercial academy. It operates as a community institution — training visiting students, yes, but primarily training children from the surrounding Pelourinho neighborhood, fully sponsored. Mestre Moraes has been doing this for over forty years. He is one of the most respected living Mestres of Capoeira Angola on earth. He is also, by all accounts, one of the most demanding teachers — not in terms of physical difficulty, but in terms of honesty. He wants the conversation in the roda to be real. If it isn't, he stops the game and shows you why.

The instructors at GCAP who work alongside Mestre Moraes have trained with him for decades. They carry the same tradition. When the Mestre is watching from the edge of the roda, everyone — instructors included — plays more carefully.

What the training looks like

Sessions begin on the floor of GCAP's academy in the Pelourinho — a room whose walls are covered with photographs of Mestre Pastinha, of rodas going back to the 1950s, of children who trained here and became Mestres themselves. The floor is hardwood and worn. The berimbau hangs on the wall. You start with the ginga.

The ginga is everything. It is the constant swaying movement that keeps you in motion, out of a fixed stance, always presenting a moving target. It is also the medium through which all expression happens in capoeira — your entire personality is visible in your ginga. Are you relaxed or tense? Playful or aggressive? Honest or dishonest? The Mestre reads all of it. On the first day he will show you how the ginga looks when someone is afraid. He will also show you how it looks when someone is home.

From the ginga, the sessions build through the fundamental movements: the au (cartwheel, which is also an evasion), the ginga-esquiva (duck-and-weave combinations), the martelo (hammer kick), the meia-lua (crescent kick), and the rasteira (leg sweep — the most important and most misunderstood movement in Angola, which requires you to read your partner's weight shift before their foot is even in the air). The berimbau is present throughout every session, setting pace and energy.

On the berimbau: The berimbau is not background music. It is the conductor of the roda. A fast berimbau means a fast, aggressive game. A slow berimbau means a slow, close, deceptive game. One note means stop. Two notes means change partners. The practitioners sing in call-and-response as the game unfolds. If you don't understand the berimbau, you don't understand what's happening. Day 4 includes a dedicated berimbau lesson.

Foundation
Meia-lua. Martelo. The kicks that built capoeira for 400 years.

First full double-session day. Morning: the meia-lua de frente (front crescent kick — the most fundamental attack in capoeira Angola), the martelo (hammer kick from a standing position), and how they are set up through the ginga. Nothing is thrown from a static position. Every kick emerges from movement and returns to movement. This is what makes capoeira Angola impossible to defend against with traditional martial arts training — there is no chambering, no telegraphing, no wind-up.

Evening: Mercado Modelo on the lower city waterfront, one of the oldest marketplaces in Brazil. The bay at sunset. This is the water where the slave ships arrived. 04 The Music
Berimbau Day
AM Training
Berimbau Lesson
Candomblé Evening
Culture + Encounter
You cannot understand the roda until you understand the berimbau.

Morning training: the rasteira — the leg sweep that defines Capoeira Angola. The rasteira is not a brute-force takedown. It is a theft: you wait for the moment your partner's weight is committed forward, then you remove the foot they were about to step onto. It requires reading the other person's balance before they themselves have committed to it. It is the most Angolan thing in capoeira — all patience and perception, disguised as something casual.

Afternoon: dedicated berimbau lesson with a GCAP instructor. How to hold it, how to strike the wire, how the different notes are produced — the open tone (solto), the closed buzz (preso), the high tone. What each combination of notes communicates in the roda. How to read what the berimbau is asking for. You will play berimbau in the roda on Day 10.

Evening: observation of a Candomblé ceremony at a terreiro in Salvador — one of the African-Brazilian religious traditions that shares roots with capoeira. Candomblé is not a tourist performance. The access is through GCAP's community relationships. Observe quietly. No photos. The orixás (spirits) have nothing to do with your phone.

Take the ginga to the sand. Then cook Bahian food with the people who invented it.

Morning: Praia do Porto da Barra — Salvador's most beloved urban beach, where the calmer inner-bay waters meet the ocean. The GCAP instructors train here sometimes; the sand changes everything. On sand, the ginga requires more force. The au demands more commitment. Falling is softer but recovering is harder. The beach session is also visible — passers-by stop to watch. The Real Thing
The roda begins. The berimbau dictates. The circle watches. Two people talk.

First live roda at GCAP. This is not a demonstration. This is the actual practice: practitioners form a circle, the berimbau and atabaque drum set the energy, two people enter from the foot of the berimbau, bow, and begin the conversation. You play with an instructor who will calibrate the game to what you know — but will not go easy in the way that makes it false. The roda has its own ethics. Disrespecting a partner, playing too aggressively, refusing to acknowledge a good move — these are violations the circle notices.

What the roda teaches that drills cannot: how to stay present when someone is actually responding to you. In drills, you already know what's coming. In the roda, you don't. The ginga either happens or it doesn't. You either see the rasteira coming or you don't. There is no simulation of this feeling. You have to be inside it.

Rest + City
No training. Salvador is a city and today you live in it.

No training today. Rio Vermelho neighborhood — a fishing village that became a bohemian district, walking distance from the beach. The Mercado de Peixe (fish market) opens at dawn: tuna the size of a door being unloaded from boats while the orixás are honored with flowers in the water. Praia de Amaralina for a swim — rougher surf than Porto da Barra, facing the open Atlantic. Lunch at a beachside barraca: fresh fish, ice-cold Brahma, your feet in the sand.

The rest day is not tourism. It is recovery — the body needs it after six days of twice-daily training. It is also context: you are training in a living city with a specific history, and the only way to understand that context is to be inside it. Move slowly today. Eat well. Sleep long.

Why rest is non-negotiable: Capoeira Angola demands a specific kind of body awareness — the ability to be loose and responsive, not tight and mechanical. That quality disappears when you're fatigued. The rest day is what allows the second week of training to build on the first, rather than just degrading it.
08 Reading
Partner Work
AM + PM Training
Advanced Roda
Night Pelourinho
Depth
Stop thinking about what to do. Start reading what's actually happening.

The Mestre identifies one thing to fix in each person's game — and it will be the same thing in different forms: you are planning instead of reading. In Capoeira Angola, planning is a liability. The moment you decide what kick you're going to throw, your partner has already felt it in your body. The Mestre's correction is consistent and patient: slow down, watch the hips, wait. The kick will present itself.

Extended partner drills focused on reading weight transfer — the fundamental skill the rasteira demands. Evening: the Pelourinho at night. Street rodas happen spontaneously in the squares. Find one. Watch how different from the academy it looks — faster, showier, less Angola, more performance. Notice what the berimbau is doing. Notice what the academic training has taught you to see that you couldn't have seen a week ago.

Service + Giving Back
Train with the children. Contribute to something that was here before you arrived.

GCAP has run a youth program in the Pelourinho for over forty years — training children from the surrounding neighborhood, fully sponsored, in the same tradition Mestre Moraes received from Mestre Pastinha. These children train seriously. Some of them will become instructors. Some will become Mestres. Today you train alongside them.

The session is led by the GCAP instructors. You warm up together. You practice the ginga together. The children will be watching how you move — with the unselfconscious scrutiny children have — and some of them will correct you, silently, by doing it better. That's not embarrassing. That's the whole point of being here: to be in a place where the tradition is real and living, and to add your presence to it, and to leave something behind in the room.

Lunch with the GCAP community. Afternoon: free time in the Pelourinho. Return to the academy in the evening for a final debrief with an instructor about what you observed in the children's training versus your own.

Why service is not optional
Capoeira survived because communities passed it on. You are inside that chain.

Mestre Pastinha spent his final years fighting to preserve Capoeira Angola when the world wanted something flashier. Mestre Moraes spent forty years building GCAP so the tradition could continue to the next generation. The children in the youth program are that next generation. When you train alongside them, you are not doing charity — you are participating in the mechanism of transmission that kept this art alive through slavery, prohibition, and modernization. That is not a small thing to be part of, even for one day.

Closing
The last roda. You play berimbau. Then you play in the circle. The Mestre watches.

Morning: full technical review — every movement learned, every combination, at speed, with a partner who will push back. The Mestre does final individual corrections: one thing, clearly stated, to carry home. In the afternoon: rest, stretch, pack. The city in the afternoon light.

Evening: the closing roda at GCAP. The full ceremony: berimbau tuned and hanging, atabaque drum, the GCAP instructors and Mestre present. Each group member plays at least one game in the roda — against an instructor calibrated to challenge exactly what they know. Some group members play berimbau, conducting the game they're not in. The Mestre sits at the foot of the berimbau and watches. When he nods, it means something you will understand by now. Departure the following morning.
